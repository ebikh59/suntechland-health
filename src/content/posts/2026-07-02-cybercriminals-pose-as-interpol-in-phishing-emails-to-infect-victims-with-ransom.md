A phishing campaign is underway where cybercriminals are posing as Interpol, targeting small businesses across the globe, including those in Europe, Asia, the Middle East, and North America. The attackers send emails claiming to be from the 'Cybercrime Investigation Unit' at Interpol, stating that the recipient has potentially been involved in suspicious or fraudulent activity. The email prompts the victim to urgently open a file, which is stored in a Proton Drive and protected by a password, both provided in the initial phishing email. This file, disguised as a video, is actually an executable that compromises the system with ransomware when run. The ransom note does not include a specific demand but instead instructs the victim to contact the attackers through a peer-to-peer private messaging service. This tactic allows the attackers to negotiate the ransom based on the size of the organization and the value of its data. Australian healthcare providers should be vigilant, as this campaign highlights the importance of verifying all unsolicited correspondence, especially those claiming to be from law enforcement agencies. Under the Privacy Act 1988 and the Australian Privacy Principles, healthcare providers have a responsibility to protect sensitive patient information from such threats. The Notifiable Data Breaches scheme also requires entities to notify affected individuals and the Office of the Australian Information Commissioner in the event of a data breach. To mitigate such risks, following the ASD Essential Eight and ACSC guidance is crucial. The use of social engineering tactics by attackers underscores the need for ongoing staff training and awareness programs. Furthermore, the simplicity of the ransomware used in this campaign does not diminish its potential impact, and healthcare providers should ensure their systems are up to date and patched against known vulnerabilities. Given the potential for significant disruption and data loss, Australian healthcare providers must remain alert to these types of phishing campaigns and take proactive steps to secure their systems and protect patient data.

## What Australian Healthcare Organisations Should Do

- Implement regular staff training on identifying and responding to phishing emails, including those that may appear to come from legitimate law enforcement agencies
- Ensure all systems and software are up to date with the latest security patches, following the ASD Essential Eight mitigation strategies
- Conduct regular security audits and penetration testing to identify vulnerabilities that could be exploited by ransomware attacks, and have an incident response plan in place that aligns with ACSC guidance and the Notifiable Data Breaches scheme
