The medical device industry, valued at around $570 billion in 2025 and expected to surpass $600 billion in 2026, is a rapidly growing segment of the global economy. As this industry expands, so does the importance of medical device compliance, referring to the regulatory obligations manufacturers must meet to ensure devices are safe, effective, and properly monitored. For Australian healthcare providers, compliance with such regulations is crucial, not just for the devices they use but also in terms of the data these devices collect and transmit, which must be handled in accordance with the Privacy Act 1988 and the Australian Privacy Principles. The Notifiable Data Breaches (NDB) scheme also plays a significant role, as any breach involving medical device data must be reported. Given the inherent risks and the critical nature of the data involved, Australian healthcare providers must be vigilant about the security of medical devices, adhering to guidelines such as the ASD Essential Eight to mitigate cyber threats. The Australian Cyber Security Centre (ACSC) provides guidance on securing medical devices, emphasizing the need for robust security measures to protect against increasingly sophisticated cyber threats. Moreover, the Security of Critical Infrastructure (SOCI) Act highlights the importance of securing critical infrastructure, including healthcare services and their associated medical devices. Ensuring compliance with these regulations and guidelines is not only a legal requirement but also essential for protecting patient health and safety. As the medical device industry continues to grow, Australian healthcare providers must stay informed about the latest regulatory requirements and cybersecurity best practices to maintain the trust and integrity of healthcare services. This includes understanding the implications of the SOCI Act on their operations and ensuring that all medical devices are securely integrated into their healthcare networks. By prioritizing compliance and cybersecurity, Australian healthcare providers can mitigate risks associated with medical devices and contribute to a safer, more secure healthcare environment. The financial and reputational consequences of non-compliance can be severe, making proactive management of medical device security and compliance indispensable for all healthcare organisations.

## What Australian Healthcare Organisations Should Do

- Implement the ASD Essential Eight mitigation strategies to protect medical devices from cyber threats
- Regularly review and update compliance with the Privacy Act 1988 and the Australian Privacy Principles, particularly in relation to data collected and transmitted by medical devices
- Conduct thorough risk assessments of all medical devices in use, following ACSC guidance, to identify and mitigate potential security vulnerabilities
