Ambient AI scribes — tools that listen to a consultation and draft the clinical note — have moved from novelty to normal in Australian general practice faster than most practice policies have kept up. The pitch is compelling: less time typing, more time with the patient, and a reduction in the documentation burden that drives clinician burnout.

The privacy questions underneath are less comfortable, and they are not optional. A scribe records a clinical conversation. That recording is health information, which is sensitive information under the Privacy Act 1988 and attracts the highest protections in the Australian Privacy Principles.

Here are the three things Australian practices need to have settled before the next consult.

## 1. Consent is not implied, and silence is not consent

The RACGP position is that clinicians must obtain patient consent before using an AI scribe, and AHPRA has emphasised informed consent for any tool that records a consultation. "Informed" is doing real work in that sentence. It means the patient is told:

- that an AI tool will be listening to or processing the consultation
- what it does with what it hears
- where the data is processed and stored
- that they can decline without any effect on their care

That last point matters most. Consent obtained in a way that makes refusal feel awkward or costly is not meaningful consent.

Practically, there are two workable models. **Verbal consent** suits established patients and intermittent use — the clinician states at the start of the consult what the tool is and offers the patient the choice. **Written consent at enrolment** suits practices where scribe use is standard and the enrolment pack already contains a privacy document. Whichever you choose, record that consent was obtained.

## 2. Find out where the audio actually goes

The question practices most often skip: does this vendor process or store data offshore, and can they reuse it?

Health information sent to a scribe vendor is a disclosure. You need to know the answer to each of these before signing:

- Is audio or transcript data processed or stored outside Australia?
- How long is the recording retained, and is it deleted after the note is generated?
- Can the vendor use your patients' consultations to improve or train their models?
- What happens to the data if the vendor is acquired or shuts down?

That fourth question catches people out. Health data has a long tail, and a vendor's insolvency is not a scenario most procurement checklists cover.

## 3. The clinician still owns the note

An AI-drafted note is a draft. The GP remains responsible for the accuracy of the clinical record and must review every note before it is finalised. Ambient scribes are good at fluent prose and imperfect at clinical nuance — they can omit a hedge, invent a tidy summary of an untidy conversation, or confidently mis-hear a medication name.

The Australian Commission on Safety and Quality in Health Care has published an AI safety scenario specifically covering ambient scribes, which is worth reading before deployment rather than after an incident.

## What a practice-level policy should cover

Individual clinician judgement is not a governance model. RACGP guidance points practice owners toward a written policy, and at minimum it should name:

- which AI tools are approved for use, and which are explicitly not
- how consent is obtained and recorded
- who reviews and signs off the generated note
- how a suspected privacy breach is escalated — and who decides whether it is notifiable under the NDB scheme
- a review date, because this technology is not sitting still

## The uncomfortable part

None of this is an argument against AI scribes. The documentation burden in Australian general practice is real, and tools that reduce it have genuine clinical value.

But a consultation is one of the most private conversations a person has, and a scribe turns it into data that leaves the room. The obligations that attach to that data did not change because the tool is new. If a practice cannot answer where the audio goes, who consented, and who checked the note, it has not deployed a productivity tool — it has quietly created a disclosure pathway that nobody is monitoring.
